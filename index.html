01 — Motivation & Research

Why BetaUp?

Understanding the problem space, the literature, existing tools, and the people we are designing for.

The Why — Motivation Statement

We chose rock climbing as our research focus because climbing uniquely combines physical challenge, technical skill, and social interaction. Unlike running or general fitness, climbing demands route-reading, movement planning, and mental composure — creating richer user needs around session logging, technique analysis, progress feedback, and community connection.

Since climbing's Olympic debut at Tokyo 2020, the sport has attracted a growing wave of younger participants. Yet the digital ecosystem still largely prioritises utility over experience — existing apps focus on training records or route databases, while fun, engagement, and playfulness remain underserved.

Several team members are regular gym climbers at XJTLU's campus wall. We found ourselves juggling multiple apps, spreadsheets, and WeChat group chats for what should be one coherent experience — log a send, celebrate progress, get coach feedback, and find routes nearby. BetaUp was built from that frustration, filling the gap between pure utility and genuinely playful daily engagement.

Competitive Analysis — Commercial Products

Vertical-Life

Training / Logbook / Topo · vertical-life.info
Strengths
  • Clear training orientation for goal-driven climbers
  • Strong progress tracking through ascents and training plans
  • Combines logbook, topo, and route sharing in one product
Gaps
  • Serious tone can be off-putting for casual climbers
  • No daily playful feedback loop
  • Users open it to train and record, not for enjoyment

Strengths
  • Powerful crag and route search for trip planning
  • Useful filters by location and grade range
  • Efficient topo experience with low interaction overhead
Gaps
  • Purely utility-driven rather than playful
  • Single-task context makes daily habit formation difficult
  • No achievement chain, social task, or growth narrative

KAYA

Social + Gym Guide + Challenges · kayaclimb.com
Strengths
  • Strong social layer with partner finding and beta videos
  • Monthly challenges and leagues encourage return visits
  • Closest benchmark to a fun climbing app
Gaps
  • Fun depends heavily on active external events
  • Lacks lightweight daily gamification
  • Engagement can drop when challenges or friends are absent

Mountain Project

Route Database / Community Guide · mountainproject.com
Strengths
  • Comprehensive routes, photos, guides, and forum content
  • High community value for beta lookup and planning
  • Classic platform with proven practical utility
Gaps
  • Feels like a reference tool, not an entertainment experience
  • Community exists, but no systematic gamification layer
  • Users open it for information, not because the app is compelling
Key Insight

All four products skew heavily towards utility — they help users climb better, find routes, or manage training data. None delivers a persistent, intrinsically enjoyable daily experience. The common pattern: engagement either depends on external events (challenges, competitions) or collapses once the immediate task is done. BetaUp targets this gap directly — placing gamification, social playfulness, and voice-first interaction at the core so every session feels like an achievement worth opening the app for.

What Users Need

User Journey Map

This user journey map depicts the full end-to-end usage experience of BetaUp's primary user, amateur bouldering climber Alex Chen. Following 5 core experience stages including pre-session awareness, function consideration, platform operation, daily service use and long-term user retention, the record systematically sorts out Alex's practical operation behaviours, real training demands, emotional changes and existing pain points in daily climbing log management. Secondary User — Coach Li Wei · Part-Time IFSC Level 1 Coach

This journey map sorts out the complete business workflow of BetaUp's secondary user, part-time professional climbing coach Li Wei. In accordance with the full cycle from daily management distress awareness, platform function understanding, account permission onboarding, routine teaching service execution to long-term tool stickiness cultivation, it analyses the coach's actual working behaviours, professional management needs, emotional fluctuations and industry pain points under the dual pressure of daily job and part-time teaching. The content reflects the widespread shortcomings of traditional coach-student management methods, including scattered student data, inefficient manual statistics, delayed remote feedback and lack of standardised milestone certification. Combined with matching platform functional opportunities, it demonstrates how BetaUp builds a closed, efficient professional teaching management system, realising digital, lightweight and systematic student progress management for climbing coaches.

Three Must-Have Playful Features
01
🏅
Badge Collection System
The platform contains four categories of badges: gym-specific badges, achievement badges, training log badges and social interaction badges. Users can unlock badges automatically after completing corresponding behaviours. Exclusive venue badges are available for local climbing gyms, while other badges reward continuous check-ins, completed training records and community engagement. All unlocked badges are displayed on personal profile pages. User research shows that badge rewards are the most popular feature among all user groups. It solves the lack of long-term positive feedback, turns invisible skill improvement into visible collectible goals, and greatly improves user continuous participation and motivation.
Playfulness: immediate positive reinforcement · social display · collection drive
02
💬
Community Post & Social Interaction
Users can publish image posts, climbing partner notices and route strategy guides on the community page. Other users can like, comment and interact with shared content, and tag coaches for professional advice. Users can also visit each other's personal pages to view training records and badge collections. It replaces scattered informal communication on social software, builds a vertical professional climbing community, turns isolated individual training into interactive shared experience, and helps climbers make friends and gain recognition from peers.
Playfulness: peer recognition · shared progress · vertical climbing community
03
🐼
AI Voice Assistant Logging
After each training session, users can simply tap the microphone icon and speak freely about their climbing details. The intelligent voice system automatically recognises professional climbing terms and generates standard structured training logs without manual typing. This feature effectively solves the common pain point where users are too exhausted to record sessions after training. It reduces recording time significantly, lowers operation friction, and makes routine training logging more convenient and enjoyable.
Playfulness: hands-free convenience · conversational interaction · instant feedback

Design Alternatives — Key Decisions
HCI Decision
Manual Form Entry AI Voice Assistant BLE Heart Rate Demo

Manual form entry is easy to implement but unsuitable mid-climb when hands are occupied. We chose an AI voice assistant — powered by STT and DeepSeek LLM — as the primary hands-free input method. Beyond voice, we also built a BLE heart rate monitoring demo that connects to an Apple Watch or compatible wearable, streaming real-time biometric data into the session view. This demonstrates our vision for deeper hardware integration and quantified training feedback.

Platform Decision
Pure Website Flutter Mobile + Web

The simple web version has many limited functions in actual use, lacking the function of taking photos and notifications, and considering that almost no one climbs with a laptop, we finally chose Flutter for its cross-platform capabilities.

Mechanic Decision
Badges + XP Points Random Loot Boxes

Compared with the clear medal acquisition mechanism, although the random reward box has a sense of surprise, it may not meet the expectations of some users. Additionally, considering that our project is a sports project, there is more motivation with specific goals, so we finally choose the plan of upgrading medals and experience points.

Testing with

System Usability Scale scores, task performance data, qualitative findings, and iterative improvements driven by user feedback.

Usability Testing — Methodology

Three participants completed a think-aloud usability session testing the live BetaUp prototype. Each session lasted approximately 15 minutes. Participants were observed performing four representative tasks: using the AI Voice Training Log, exploring the Progress Dashboard, interacting with the Badge Reward System, and posting in the Community Module. A 10-question System Usability Scale (SUS) questionnaire was administered after each session. Informed consent was obtained from all participants prior to data collection.

Key Findings

Issues Identified

  • P1AI Voice Training Log had the lowest completion rate (66.7%) and highest error count (1.3 avg) — users needed clearer interaction guidance to activate and use the voice feature
  • P2Community Module required the longest completion time (242s) — navigation flow between post creation and feed browsing was not immediately intuitive

Positive Feedback

  • Participants generally found core functions understandable and easy to learn across all four task areas
  • Progress Visualization Dashboard received the clearest positive feedback — 100% completion in just 22 seconds with zero errors
  • The AI Voice Training Log was considered genuinely useful despite the interaction friction — participants expressed interest in the concept and wanted to use it more

06 — AI & Technical Reflection

AI-Assisted
Development

Our Vibe Coding process, the tools we used, and an honest account of what worked and what fell short.

PROCESS
Our Vibe Coding Approach

BetaUp was built with AI-assisted development as a first-class workflow throughout the entire project — from requirement gathering to final polish. Rather than using AI as a passive code generator, we operated as experienced Vibe Coders: deliberately designing prompts with precise context (role, constraints, expected output format), invoking specialised skills for specific tasks, and dispatching parallel agents for independent workstreams.

At the ideation stage, a brainstorming skill was used to explore feature directions before committing to requirements. During frontend development, the impeccable skill drove design decisions — colour strategy, typography hierarchy, component layout — while a frontend-design skill handled component-level implementation. After completing major features, a requesting-code-review skill was used to catch issues before moving forward. Independent tasks — backend endpoint development, Flutter screen implementation, portfolio redesign — were dispatched to parallel agents simultaneously, with agents also used cross-functionally to review each other's output. This multi-agent, skill-augmented workflow meant the team could move faster without sacrificing quality or coherence.

REFLECTION
What Worked — and What Didn't

Effective

  • Claude Code + skills ecosystem was highly effective across the full stack. Spring Boot endpoint scaffolding, Flutter widget generation, and JWT auth boilerplate were all reliable enough to integrate with minimal rework — largely because prompts were designed with precise context from the start.
  • DeepSeek performed reliably as the voice assistant NLP engine for Chinese climbing commands. After 5+ prompt iterations, structured JSON output (LOG_CLIMB, START_SESSION, QUERY_STATS) was stable and predictable.
  • Parallel multi-agent execution meaningfully compressed development time — backend and frontend workstreams ran concurrently without blocking each other.
  • Codex provided reliable automated code review before key merges, catching edge cases that manual review missed. Antigravity was useful for targeted UI micro-optimisations on the portfolio page.

Fell Short

  • 豆包 / 可灵 generated video clips with physically unrealistic climbing movements — AI models lack domain understanding of climbing biomechanics. The footage required heavy manual editing and was ultimately used only as background material rather than primary demonstration content.
  • DeepSeek showed noticeably weaker performance on English commands, reflecting its training data distribution. This is a documented limitation: non-Chinese speakers may find the voice assistant less reliable.
  • ChatGPT was less precise than Claude Code for specific coding tasks and required more correction rounds — better suited to content and ideation than implementation.

Ethical Considerations

Accessibility

All AI-generated UI components were checked for contrast ratios (minimum 4.5:1 for body text). The voice assistant is a supplementary input method — every voice action is also reachable via touch UI.

Language Bias

The DeepSeek NLP model performs better on Chinese commands due to training data distribution. Non-Chinese speakers may experience lower voice recognition accuracy — documented as a known limitation.

Data Privacy

No voice audio is stored. Transcribed text is sent to DeepSeek's API for processing; users are informed via the microphone permissions dialogue before first use. Session data is stored only in our local H2 database.

Code Ownership

AI-generated code was reviewed, understood, and refactored where needed before integration. The developer remains responsible for correctness, security (JWT, SQL safety), and alignment with user requirements.
