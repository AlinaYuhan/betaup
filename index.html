About the Project

What is
BetaUp?

BetaUp is a human-centric application designed to enhance the experience of climbers and coaches through gamification, real-time feedback, and social connectivity.

Rooted in the Active Lifestyles theme of CPT208, our project explores how playful design principles can motivate climbers from beginner to advanced level, and help coaches deliver more engaging, data-driven guidance.

We apply a user-centred design process: conducting field research, building personas, prototyping iteratively, and evaluating with real users at climbing venues.

01 — Motivation & Research

Why BetaUp?

Understanding the problem space, the literature, existing tools, and the people we are designing for.

The Why — Motivation Statement

Rock climbing has evolved from a niche pursuit into one of the fastest-growing fitness activities worldwide, gaining its Olympic debut at Tokyo 2020 and attracting over 35 million participants globally. Yet the digital ecosystem for climbers remains surprisingly fragmented: route databases such as 27crags catalogue walls but offer no community dynamics; training apps like Crimpd optimise strength workouts but ignore the social dimension; general fitness platforms like Strava were never designed for climbing's unique grading systems and venue culture.

Several of our team members are regular gym climbers at XJTLU's campus wall. We found ourselves juggling three different apps, a spreadsheet, and WhatsApp group chats to manage what should be one coherent experience — log a send, celebrate with friends, receive coach feedback, and discover new routes nearby. BetaUp was built from that frustration.

By placing gamification, coach communication, and social connectivity at its core, BetaUp makes training sessions genuinely enjoyable and progress immediately visible. The project aligns with CPT208's Active Lifestyles theme by applying human-centric design principles to reduce friction for climbers of all levels — turning every session into a small, satisfying achievement, whether it is a first V2 send or closing a V8 project that has been open for months.

Literature Review — Academic Papers
4 Academic Papers · 3 Strengths + 3 Gaps per source
Paper 3 Strengths 3 Gaps
Hamari, Koivisto & Sarsa (2014)
Does Gamification Work? A Literature Review of Empirical Studies on Gamification HICSS 2014 DOI: 10.1109/HICSS.2014.377
  • Strong empirical meta-analysis across 24 peer-reviewed studies
  • Identifies badges, leaderboards, and points as primary engagement drivers
  • Broad applicability across health, education, and commerce domains
  • No climbing-specific or extreme-sport context data
  • Most studies span < 3 months — no longitudinal evidence
  • Does not address negative effects of over-gamification (burnout, extrinsic pressure)
Deterding, Dixon, Khaled & Nacke (2011)
From Game Design Elements to Gamefulness: Defining "Gamification" MindTrek 2011 DOI: 10.1145/2181037.2181040
  • Defines foundational vocabulary still cited in 12,000+ papers (PBL framework)
  • Clearly distinguishes gamification from serious games and playful design
  • Rigorous taxonomy applicable across any interactive system
  • No empirical user study — purely conceptual framework
  • Provides no HCI evaluation or measurement guidelines
  • Predates mobile-first and wearable design eras
Consolvo, McDonald & Landay (2009)
Theory-driven Design Strategies for Technologies that Support Behaviour Change CHI 2009 DOI: 10.1145/1518701.1518766
  • 8 actionable design strategies derived from a real-world user study (20 participants)
  • Covers abstract and ambient feedback patterns relevant to health app design
  • Strong HCI methodological framing grounded in social learning theory
  • Mobile-first design considerations entirely absent — pre-smartphone era
  • Does not address competitive gamification or social comparison
  • Focuses on individual behaviour change, ignoring coach-athlete dynamics
Rooksby, Rost, Morrison & Chalmers (2014)
Personal Tracking as Lived Informatics CHI 2014 DOI: 10.1145/2556288.2557039
  • Rich qualitative diary study with 22 participants revealing real tracking habits
  • Identifies 5 distinct personal tracking styles (directive, aesthetic, documentary…)
  • Reveals common failure modes of data-driven apps that feel like chores
  • Active sports context absent — most data from steps, sleep, and calories
  • No design recommendations specific to skill-based sports like climbing
  • Coach or trainer perspective completely missing from analysis
Competitive Analysis — Commercial Products
4 Commercial Benchmarks · 3 Strengths + 3 Gaps per product
Product 3 Strengths 3 Gaps
27crags
Route database & climbing log 27crags.com
  • Largest global route database (700k+ routes, 140+ countries)
  • Clean offline map and route quality ratings from the community
  • Trusted and used by competition climbing circuits worldwide
  • No gamification, badge system, or progress celebration
  • No coach-athlete relationship or feedback workflow
  • Social feed is minimal and text-only — no community feel
Crimpd
Strength & fingerboard training crimpd.com
  • Professional fingerboard and hangboard training plans from certified coaches
  • Structured progressive overload programming with clear visual guides
  • Clean, distraction-free onboarding for serious training
  • No route logging or session tracking for gym climbing
  • No community, social feed, or leaderboard features
  • Only useful to climbers who own hang-board equipment
Strava
General sports & running social network strava.com
  • Industry-leading achievement and badge system with 60+ activity types
  • Seamless GPS segment leaderboards and kudos social graph (100M+ users)
  • Clean activity feed with photos, comments, and group challenges
  • No climbing-specific grading (V-scale, French grades, YDS)
  • GPS segments meaningless for indoor bouldering sessions
  • No coach certification workflow or athlete management
TopLogger
Competition climbing management toplogger.nu
  • Live competition scoring and real-time leaderboard used by IFSC events
  • Powerful venue operator tools for route setting and comp management
  • Multi-discipline support (lead, boulder, speed) with judge workflows
  • Far too complex for everyday recreational training use cases
  • No personal training narrative, progress history, or achievement system
  • No AI features, voice interaction, or social community layer

Three Must-Have Playful Features
01
🏅
Achievement & Badge System
Climbers automatically unlock achievement badges upon reaching milestones: first send, 10 sessions completed, first V5, 7-day streak, and 15 others. Each unlock triggers an animated celebration with a push notification. Badges are displayed publicly on the user's profile, creating an aspirational showcase visible to the community.
Playfulness: immediate positive reinforcement · social display · collection drive
02
📊
Community Session Leaderboard
Every training session contributes to a live community leaderboard ranked by sends, highest grade, or XP earned. Users see global and follower-only rankings in real time, with position changes animated. A weekly "Top Climber" badge is awarded to the session leader, renewing competitive motivation each week.
Playfulness: competitive ranking · social comparison · weekly reward cycle
03
🐼
AI Voice Assistant (Panda)
A draggable panda avatar activates a full-duplex voice interface powered by DeepSeek NLP and Web Speech API. Climbers speak naturally mid-session: "Log a V5 flash on the blue slab" or "Start my session at Campus Wall." The panda replies in Chinese, confirms the action, and saves data without touching the screen.
Playfulness: delightful character design · hands-free friction removal · conversational loop

Platform Decision
Pure Web SPA
Rejected		 Fast prototype; no native device APIs; poor offline capability.

06 — Technical Reflection

AI-Assisted
Development

How we used large language model tools, what we generated, how we verified it, and the ethical considerations.

AI TOOL [1]
Claude Code — Anthropic
claude.ai · accessed April 2026

Used for: Spring Boot API scaffolding (controller, service, repository layers), Flutter screen component generation, JWT authentication boilerplate, database schema design, DeepSeek system prompt engineering, and the voice assistant pipeline — including the VoiceService state machine, DeepSeekClient HTTP wrapper, and the custom CustomPainter panda widget.

Verification approach: Every generated backend endpoint was manually tested via browser or Postman before integration into the Flutter client. Flutter screens were hot-reloaded and exercised on Chrome. Voice assistant state machine edge cases (simultaneous TTS + STT, network failure during DeepSeek call, empty transcripts) were tested by simulating each failure mode.
AI TOOL [2]
DeepSeek Chat API — DeepSeek
api.deepseek.com · accessed April 2026

Used as: The NLP engine inside the voice assistant, parsing natural-language Chinese climbing commands into structured JSON actions — LOG_CLIMB, START_SESSION, END_SESSION, and QUERY_STATS — with typed parameters (grade, venue, result, attempts, notes).

Verification approach: The system prompt was iterated 5+ times to improve robustness. Edge cases explicitly tested: partial commands ("log V5"), ambiguous grades ("blue route"), requests outside app scope ("book a gym slot"), multi-turn context (asking about a session that was just started), and Chinese/English mixed input.

Ethical Considerations

Accessibility

All AI-generated UI components were checked for contrast ratios (minimum 4.5:1 for body text). The voice assistant is a supplementary input method — every voice action is also reachable via touch UI, so the app remains fully usable for users in noisy environments or with speech difficulties.

Language Bias

The DeepSeek NLP model performs better on Chinese commands due to its training data distribution. We document this limitation clearly: non-Chinese speakers may experience lower voice recognition accuracy. A future iteration would support English commands equally.

Data Privacy

No voice audio is stored by BetaUp. Transcribed text is sent to DeepSeek's API for NLP processing; users are informed of this via the microphone permissions dialogue before first use. All session and climb data is stored only in our local H2 database instance.

Code Ownership

AI-generated code was reviewed, understood, and in many cases refactored by the lead developer before integration. We do not submit AI output as-is. The developer is responsible for correctness, security (JWT, SQL safety), and alignment with user requirements.
